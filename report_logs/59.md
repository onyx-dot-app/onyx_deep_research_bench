ANSWER:
## 1) What “precise navigation” means in bird migration: **compass** + **map** + **movement ecology**
Migrating birds solve two linked problems:

1) **Direction-keeping (“compass orientation”)** – maintaining an appropriate heading (e.g., SW in autumn) despite clouds, changing celestial geometry, magnetic declination, and crosswinds. This can be expressed even in captivity as seasonally appropriate *migratory restlessness* and is commonly measured in orientation cages such as **Emlen funnels** [[1]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5513225/).

2) **Location-finding (“map” or position fixing / true navigation)** – determining where they are relative to a goal (breeding site, wintering area, migratory corridor, or key stopover), especially when displaced or off-route. This is classically demonstrated by **true navigation**: compensating after displacement from unfamiliar sites [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[15]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11285401/)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).

In the real world, birds must also manage a third layer: **aerodynamic and ecological constraints** (winds, barriers, stopover availability, predation risk, fueling state). So “precision” is not only sensory accuracy—it is also *adaptive decision-making under uncertainty* (when to depart, how much to drift, where to refuel) [[51]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4754638/)[[52]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3431116/)[[56]](https://research.fs.usda.gov/treesearch/download/22889.pdf).

## 2) How birds keep direction: the major **compass cues** and what we know from experiments

### A. **Geomagnetic compass** (widely available, works under clouds)
**Key functional properties**
- In many species, the magnetic compass is an **inclination compass**: it detects the *axis* of the field lines (poleward vs equatorward) rather than magnetic polarity (north vs south) [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4264356/)[[10]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2021.667000/full)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- It is **light-dependent** in typical migratory songbird compass orientation: usually functional under short-wavelength light (UV–green) and disrupted under longer wavelengths (yellow/red) [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4264356/)[[10]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2021.667000/full)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- It shows a **“functional window”** in intensity: moderate intensity shifts can cause disorientation unless birds adapt through experience [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4264356/)[[10]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2021.667000/full)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).

**Core evidence**
- If magnetic north is rotated using coil systems, many migrants rotate their preferred direction accordingly in orientation tests—classic evidence for a magnetic compass [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4264356/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- **Radiofrequency (RF) fields** can disrupt magnetic-compass orientation, consistent with a radical-pair mechanism (see Mechanisms, below) [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4264356/)[[11]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8918455/).

**Ecological role**
- Especially valuable when celestial cues are unavailable (overcast, fog, below cloud deck, nocturnal migration without clear stars) and as a stable reference over short timescales.

---

### B. **Sun compass** (mostly diurnal, but also used for calibration and in some nocturnal contexts)
**What makes it a “true sun compass”**
- Birds can use the Sun as a directional reference **with time compensation** (because the Sun’s azimuth changes through the day) [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3049006/)[[50]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4222775/).

**Evidence**
- **Clock-shift experiments**: shifting birds’ circadian clocks produces predictable deflections in their headings/vanishing bearings, consistent with time-compensated use of the Sun’s azimuth [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3049006/)[[50]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4222775/).
- In the field, clock-shifting a pelagic migrant (Manx shearwater) produced a measurable, though partial, clock-shift effect during homing, implying sun-compass input integrated with other cues (e.g., landmarks) [[4]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982217315877).

**Ecological role**
- Strong for diurnal migrants and for homing, and can also provide a reference to calibrate other compasses (directly or indirectly via twilight cues) [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3049006/)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/).

---

### C. **Polarized-light compass / twilight calibration cue**
Skylight polarization patterns are strongest and most geometrically informative near **sunrise/sunset**, and a major ecological idea is that twilight provides a robust “daily reset” for compass alignment [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/).

**Evidence for calibration**
- In white-throated sparrows, rotating the polarization pattern at sunrise/sunset by ±90° caused corresponding shifts consistent with **recalibration of the magnetic compass**, in both juveniles and adults and in both spring and autumn [[8]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/212/21/3466/19006/White-throated-sparrows-calibrate-their-magnetic).

**But: not universal**
- Multiple European cue-conflict field and standardized experiments report **no or limited recalibration** under some conditions, even when birds could see horizon and stars; only some groups (e.g., adult robins in one protocol) showed calibration [[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/)[[47]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/225/16/jeb243631/276374/Access-to-the-sky-near-the-horizon-and-stars-does).
- The current evidence base therefore supports *species-, population-, and context-dependence* rather than a single rule like “twilight always calibrates magnetism” [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/)[[47]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/225/16/jeb243631/276374/Access-to-the-sky-near-the-horizon-and-stars-does).

**Ecological role**
- Likely important as a *calibration opportunity* at stopovers and at daily transitions, but how consistently it is used varies across taxa and conditions.

---

### D. **Star compass** (mostly nocturnal migrants)
**What birds learn**
- Many nocturnal migrants use a **time-independent star compass** based on learned star-pattern geometry around the axis of celestial rotation, rather than a time-compensated “stellar clock” [[6]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/204/22/3855/32913/Migrating-songbirds-tested-in-computer-controlled).

**Evidence**
- Classic planetarium work with indigo buntings showed juveniles must **learn** the star compass: birds raised without a rotating night sky failed to orient normally; birds exposed to a rotated artificial sky oriented relative to the learned rotation axis [[5]](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.170.3963.1198).
- Planetarium/funnel experiments in other migrants support a geometric, time-independent star compass rather than true “star navigation” for position [[6]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/204/22/3855/32913/Migrating-songbirds-tested-in-computer-controlled).

**Ecological role**
- Provides a stable directional reference on clear nights; can interact with magnetic cues through calibration/conflict dynamics [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/).

### E. **Wind, drift, optic flow, and the “moving medium” problem**
Even with a perfect compass, a bird’s **ground track** can be wrong because winds push it sideways. Precision therefore includes **drift strategy**: full drift vs partial vs full compensation.

**Evidence from radar and tracking**
- Dual-polarization radar shows nocturnal songbirds often **drift** but compensate more strongly near coasts (where drift risks ocean crossing), and coastal compensation can increase through the night—suggesting dynamic risk-based decisions rather than a fixed rule [[51]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4754638/).
- Modeling + radar synthesis supports that juveniles along corridors with generally poor winds may benefit from **partial compensation** and only moderate wind selectivity; radar data are consistent with low drift tolerance and partial compensation in passerines [[52]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3431116/).
- Satellite telemetry in raptors shows strong age effects: juveniles exhibited near **full drift**, whereas adults reduced drift substantially (reported ~29% drift), consistent with experience improving drift assessment/compensation [[53]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1698035/).
- Ontogeny studies in pelagic frigatebirds similarly indicate drift reduction improves with experience and with cue availability (e.g., landmarks), implying learning in how birds fuse wind and navigation cues [[13]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7661306/).

**Ecological role**
- This is a major source of “navigation error” and “precision loss” in nature; birds often trade off energy/time against spatial accuracy depending on landscape risk (coastlines, barriers) and individual state [[51]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4754638/)[[52]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3431116/)[[54]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11843740/).

## 3) How birds find location: the **map** problem (positional cues) and the best-supported hypotheses
A useful ecological distinction is:

- **Vector navigation**: juveniles follow an inherited program (direction + duration/sequence), which can succeed without knowing geographic location [[15]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11285401/)[[44]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/199/1/49/7362/Genetic-Basis-Mode-of-Inheritance-and-Evolutionary)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).
- **True navigation**: experienced birds compensate for displacement, implying a map component [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).

### A. Age and experience: why “precise location” often improves with age
Perdeck’s classic starling translocations—reassessed with modern analysis—support a robust pattern:
- **Juveniles** displaced off-route tended not to correct, consistent with inherited vector programs.
- **Adults** corrected toward the population-specific wintering area, consistent with true navigation based on learned map information [[15]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11285401/).
Genetic work in migrants (e.g., blackcaps) also shows inherited migratory direction can be controlled by relatively few genes and can evolve rapidly, supporting the idea that first-time direction can be “pre-programmed” even when positional mapping is not [[44]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/199/1/49/7362/Genetic-Basis-Mode-of-Inheritance-and-Evolutionary).

---

### B. **Olfactory / atmospheric chemical cues** (strong support in several wild displacement studies, but with controversies in pigeons)
**Pigeons: strong tradition + active debate**
- A major body of work argues pigeons learn relationships between winds and airborne odors at home and use odor gradients after displacement as a map [[18]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4203629/)[[20]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5065961/).
- However, the “false-release-site” paradigm has been used to argue that odors may sometimes act more as an **activator** of a non-olfactory map rather than carrying map coordinates themselves; exposure to *artificial novel odors* could still trigger site-specific orientation in one study, challenging a strict odor-map interpretation under those conditions [[17]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2842632/). Reviews disagree strongly on how decisive this is [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[18]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4203629/).

**Wild migrants/seabirds: compelling tracking + sensory deprivation evidence**
- **Lesser black-backed gulls** translocated far outside their corridor: birds with **olfactory nerve section** kept a southerly migratory tendency but failed to compensate toward the corridor; intact birds (and trigeminal-section birds) compensated toward the corridor [[16]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26597351/)[[48]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep17061). This is powerful because it separates *compass direction* (still present) from *map correction* (lost without olfaction).
- **Scopoli’s shearwaters** displaced over open sea: **anosmic** birds failed to orient homeward offshore, whereas magnetically disturbed birds oriented and homed like controls; anosmic birds improved once they reached coastlines, implying a switch to visual/topographic guidance near land [[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).

**Ecological interpretation**
- Olfaction seems especially valuable:
  - over **featureless environments** (open ocean),
  - when birds must **rejoin a corridor** after displacement,
  - and potentially when familiar odor “signatures” of regions/stopovers can be recognized [[20]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5065961/)[[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).

---

### C. **Geomagnetic “map” (positional information from intensity/inclination)**
Birds clearly use a magnetic *compass*, but whether and how they use magnetic parameters as a *map* is more complex.

**What could work in principle**
- Geographic variation in **total intensity** and **inclination** could provide coarse coordinate-like information [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[33]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8918461/).
- Some taxa (across animals) show evidence consistent with **geomagnetic imprinting** on natal magnetic conditions, aiding philopatry [[33]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8918461/).

**Major limitations (ecological realism)**
- Magnetic isolines are not clean, orthogonal coordinates everywhere; regional irregularities and anomalies can make extrapolation difficult without prior experience [[18]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4203629/)[[33]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8918461/).
- Some experimental results look more like **signposts** (e.g., intensity thresholds triggering route changes/stop) rather than a full bicoordinate map [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107).

**Mixed experimental evidence in birds**
- In some seabirds, attaching strong magnets did not impair homing, which weighs against a universal magnetic-map reliance in those systems [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).
- Magnetic pulse experiments in migrants can alter adult (not juvenile) departure precision, interpreted as disrupting an experience-based component possibly linked to map information [[19]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3627120/).
- Reviews and neurobiological syntheses often propose a dual-system view: a retinal radical-pair compass + a trigeminally mediated intensity “map” channel, but acknowledge that definitive demonstrations of a global bicoordinate magnetic map in free-ranging birds remain challenging and species-dependent [[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds)[[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).

**Ecological interpretation**
- Magnetic parameters may contribute as:
  - regional familiarity cues,
  - signposts (e.g., “you are too far north”),
  - or one layer within a redundant map used alongside olfactory and visual information [[14]](https://zslpublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jzo.12107)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds)[[33]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8918461/)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).

---

### D. **Visual landmarks, topography, coastlines (local guidance and “familiar area” navigation)**
- Visual piloting is strongly supported within **familiar areas** and near coastlines/landmarks [[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486)[[43]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959438824000321).
- The shearwater experiment shows anosmic birds eventually homed by switching to **coastline-following** once land cues became available [[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).
- However, by definition, visual landmarks cannot fully explain true navigation from reliably unfamiliar locations (unless birds have learned regional features previously) [[18]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4203629/)[[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/).

**Ecological interpretation**
- Visual cues are often a high-precision system in the final stages (regional approach, corridor following, coastline tracking), and likely interact with hippocampal spatial processing (see Mechanisms) [[43]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959438824000321).

---

### E. **Infrasound and other proposed map cues (intriguing but less settled)**
- Infrasound has been proposed as a long-range “acoustic map,” potentially linked to microseisms and topography; disruptions of pigeon races were linked to infrasonic shock waves from Concorde flights in one account [[22]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/203/7/1103/8430/Infrasound-and-the-Avian-Navigational-Map).
- Pigeons can detect very low-frequency infrasound, but controlled displacement evidence establishing infrasound as a primary map cue remains limited and debated in the cited reviews [[18]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4203629/)[[22]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/203/7/1103/8430/Infrasound-and-the-Avian-Navigational-Map).

## 4) Biological mechanisms: how birds sense these cues (with emphasis on magnetoreception)

### A. Two (partly separate) magnetic sensory streams: **retinal compass** vs **trigeminal/iron-based map**
A leading synthesis proposes:
1) A **light-dependent magnetic compass** mediated by radical-pair reactions in the eye (optic nerve → visual forebrain).
2) A **magnetic-intensity pathway** likely mediated by iron-based receptors and carried by the **trigeminal system** (ophthalmic branch) [[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds)[[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/).

This matches much behavioral and neurobiological evidence, but important controversies remain (below).

---

### B. The retinal radical-pair compass: cryptochromes and Cluster N
**Cryptochrome 4 (Cry4) as a leading candidate**
- Cry4 protein is localized in the outer segments of specific cones in robin and chicken retinas, consistent with a sensory role [[35]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982217316056).
- In European robins, Cry4 mRNA expression was higher in migratory seasons than in non-migratory seasons, suggesting functional upregulation during migration (correlational but suggestive) [[35]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982217316056).
- European robin Cry4 exists in isoforms (Cry4a/Cry4b) with different rhythmic expression patterns, complicating simple “always-on receptor” assumptions and keeping open which isoform(s) matter for magnetoreception [[36]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-72579-2).
- Critically, purified European robin **CRY4 shows magnetic-field-sensitive photochemistry in vitro**, stronger than chicken/pigeon CRY4 in that report, and mutational work implicates flavin–tryptophan radical-pair chains [[37]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03618-9).

**Brain processing: Cluster N**
- In European robins, lesions of **Cluster N** abolished magnetic compass orientation while leaving sun and star compass orientation intact, implying Cluster N is necessary for magnetic-compass-guided orientation in that species [[38]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19865170/).
- Trigeminal ophthalmic nerve section did not impair magnetic compass orientation in the same robin study, consistent with the compass being vision-mediated rather than beak/trigeminal mediated [[38]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19865170/).
- Cluster N is strongly night-active in migrants and linked to visual input, though detecting magnetic-condition modulation in activity markers has methodological limits [[39]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2475547/).

---

### C. Iron-based receptors and trigeminal pathways: intensity information, but receptor anatomy disputed
**Functional evidence for trigeminal magnetic intensity input**
- Electrophysiology recorded magnetic-intensity responses in trigeminal/ophthalmic nerve units in passerines [[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- Conditioning experiments and anomaly discrimination can depend on beak integrity/trigeminal pathways [[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- In some displacement/virtual-displacement experiments, cutting trigeminal pathways prevented compensation, consistent with trigeminal magnetic input serving a map-like function [[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).

**But: receptor location and identity remain controversial**
- Putative iron-containing beak structures have been reinterpreted by some as macrophages rather than dedicated receptors, and some detection attempts failed to find the expected magnetite signatures; reviews conclude the exact receptor anatomy is unresolved despite trigeminal functional evidence [[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).

---

### D. Inner ear / vestibular magnetic sensing: possible additional pathway (not fully reconciled)
- Work in pigeons has argued for magnetic pathways involving **lagena** or vestibular structures based on c-Fos mapping and ablations [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S096098221100114X), but later syntheses emphasize conflicting behavioral results and uncertain functional interpretation [[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds).
- A more recent whole-brain activity mapping study in pigeons supports a light-independent vestibular mechanism consistent with **electromagnetic induction** in semicircular canals and identifies candidate specialized hair cells [[41]](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aea6425).
Overall: this suggests pigeons (and perhaps other birds) may possess additional magnetic sensing routes beyond the classic retinal compass, but how these map onto *migration* vs *homing*, and compass vs map tasks, remains an active research frontier [[41]](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aea6425)[[32]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/16/158/20190295/87177/Magnetoreception-in-birdsMagnetoreception-in-birds)[[34]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6769297/).

---

### E. Neural systems for “map” integration beyond magnetoreception
- Navigation-related networks in birds prominently involve **olfactory bulbs / piriform cortex** and the **hippocampal formation**, often framed as complementary systems: olfaction for unfamiliar-area navigation, hippocampus for familiar visual-landmark guidance [[43]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959438824000321).
- Thalamic nuclei (e.g., dorsal anterior thalamus / DLL in pigeons) show odor-modulated activity linked to processing outward-journey information used for later homing decisions [[42]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/behavioral-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnbeh.2017.00213/full).

## 5) How birds integrate cues in real time: hierarchy, switching, recalibration, and error correction

### A. Cue conflict and recalibration: no single universal rule
Cue-conflict experiments rotate magnetic cues relative to celestial cues and ask which system dominates and whether one is recalibrated to match the other [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/)[[47]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/225/16/jeb243631/276374/Access-to-the-sky-near-the-horizon-and-stars-does).

**Patterns reported across many studies**
- During migration, many experiments show birds often follow the **magnetic** cue when conflicts are imposed, consistent with magnetic dominance in those protocols [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues).
- Some studies support **twilight polarization** as a calibration reference for magnetic orientation [[8]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/212/21/3466/19006/White-throated-sparrows-calibrate-their-magnetic), and theoretical syntheses propose sequences like polarization → magnetic alignment → stellar alignment [[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/).

**But contradictory results are common**
- Field radio-tracking in garden warblers found **no evidence** of compass recalibration after a strong 90° sunset magnetic shift; authors discuss how phototaxis and later star-compass use could mask calibration effects [[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/).
- A standardized multi-species European study found almost no calibration across species/ages/seasons (except adult robins), even when birds had horizon and star visibility during the conflict [[47]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/225/16/jeb243631/276374/Access-to-the-sky-near-the-horizon-and-stars-does).
- Reviews emphasize that outcomes depend on *which cues are available*, experimental geometry (horizon view), control choices, and likely species/population differences [[2]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/209/1/2/33398/Calibration-of-magnetic-and-celestial-compass-cues)[[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4763052/)[[47]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/225/16/jeb243631/276374/Access-to-the-sky-near-the-horizon-and-stars-does).

**Ecological takeaway**
Birds appear to use **adaptive, context-dependent sensor fusion**. They may:
- rely on the most reliable cue *available in that moment* (e.g., magnetism under overcast),
- recalibrate at stopovers or at twilight sometimes but not always,
- and switch strategies by species, experience, and habitat (ocean vs land, coastal vs inland) [[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/)[[51]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4754638/)[[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).

---

### B. Scale-dependent navigation: “global cues” to “local cues”
A practical ecological model is that birds shift cue weighting by spatial scale:
- **Long-distance phase**: global compasses (magnetic, celestial) + broad map cues (olfactory regions, magnetic signposts).
- **Narrowing-in**: increasing use of learned regional cues and corridor landmarks.
- **Pinpointing**: fine-scale landmarks, local odors, social cues in some taxa [[46]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11900352/)[[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486)[[43]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959438824000321).

The shearwater result (anosmic birds failing offshore but succeeding once coastlines appear) is a clear real-world example of scale- and habitat-dependent cue switching [[21]](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep16486).

---

### C. Wind as a constant “disturbance” requiring continuous correction
Even absent human impacts, winds force birds to blend:
- internal compass headings,
- map-based goals,
- and risk/energy tradeoffs (drift vs compensation; departure timing based on winds) [[51]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4754638/)[[52]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3431116/)[[54]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11843740/).

This is why navigation precision is often probabilistic and state-dependent rather than a single deterministic compass bearing.

## 6) Disturbances that disrupt migratory navigation and how strong the evidence is

### A. **Artificial light at night (ALAN): disorientation, attraction, altered flight behavior, collisions**
**Behavioral disruption**
- Powerful field quantification: the NYC **Tribute in Light** caused major aggregation and circling behavior up to 4 km altitude; densities near the installation exceeded baseline by >20× routinely and sometimes >150×. Turning lights off caused rapid dispersal and normalization within minutes. Estimated ~1.1 million birds affected across 7 nights (0.6–1.6 million CI) [[24]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1708574114).

**Collision amplification**
- Building collisions kill an estimated **365–988 million birds annually in the U.S.** [[25]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8214683/).
- A 21-year dataset at McCormick Place (Chicago) found collision risk strongly predicted by migration intensity, wind, and **window light output**. A 1 SD increase in lighted window area corresponded to ~1.95× higher spring collisions and ~1.52× in fall; model predictions implied very large differences between low and high lighting regimes, and estimated ~60% mortality reduction if lighting were reduced to historically minimal levels [[25]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8214683/)[[26]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2101666118).

**Mechanisms**
- Attraction/entrapment in lit areas (positive phototaxis), loss of celestial cue visibility, and increased exposure to glass hazards once drawn into urban environments [[23]](https://lightsoutheartland.org/images/articles/Burt_TREE_2023EcolandEvolrevlighteffectsonmigration2023.pdf)[[25]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8214683/).

**Mitigation supported by evidence**
- “Lights out” and selective shutdown during high migration nights: rapid behavioral reversibility is documented at Tribute in Light [[24]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1708574114) and modeled reductions are large at McCormick Place [[25]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8214683/)[[26]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2101666118).
- Additional best practices (shielding, warmer spectra, reducing uplight) are widely recommended, though species responses can vary [[23]](https://lightsoutheartland.org/images/articles/Burt_TREE_2023EcolandEvolrevlighteffectsonmigration2023.pdf)[[57]](https://www.fws.gov/sites/default/files/documents/reducing-bird-collisions-with-buildings.pdf)[[58]](https://abcbirds.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/Misguiding-Light-The-Role-Artificial-Light-Plays-in-Bird-Mortality-from-Collisions-with-Glass.pdf).

---

### B. **Anthropogenic electromagnetic noise (EMN): magnetic compass disruption**
- A double-blind study found European robins could not use their magnetic compass in **urban electromagnetic noise** (50 kHz–5 MHz), but regained magnetic orientation in grounded, aluminum-screened huts that reduced noise by ~100×; reintroducing broadband noise removed orientation again [[27]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24805233/).
- Other RF experiments show frequency- and species-dependent outcomes (e.g., blackcaps showing no effect in 0.1–100 kHz in one study), indicating that the precise disruptive bands and field characteristics matter and may not generalize simply [[28]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6936046/).

**Ecological implication**
In human-dominated landscapes, EM noise can plausibly create “magnetic fog,” especially near sources, potentially forcing birds to rely more on celestial cues or other compasses when available—or increasing disorientation when multiple cues are degraded simultaneously (e.g., overcast + EM noise) [[27]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24805233/)[[29]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306317120).

---

### C. **Space weather / geomagnetic storms: natural degradation of magnetic reliability**
- A 22+ year radar analysis across the U.S. Great Plains found **9–17% decreases in migration intensity** during severe geomagnetic disturbance, after controlling for weather and spatiotemporal patterns [[29]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306317120).
- It also found evidence consistent with reduced “effort flying against the wind” during fall geomagnetic disturbance, especially under **overcast** conditions—suggesting compounded disruption when celestial cues are blocked and magnetic cues are disturbed [[29]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306317120).

---

### D. **Weather extremes, smoke, and visibility: navigation conditions and energetic collapse**
- Analyses of the 2020 mass mortality events in western/central North America identify **air quality and proximity to wildfire** as major predictors of reported mortality-event distributions; proposed mechanisms include respiratory stress, impaired navigation due to low visibility, and reduced foraging success [[60]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8029984/).
- Banding-based work in Utah during 2020 found relationships between burned acres and capture numbers/body condition proxies and discusses how fires could force premature departures, habitat displacement, or stopover skipping; necropsy descriptions from specimens elsewhere indicated severe emaciation in many victims [[67]](https://ace-eco.org/vol17/iss2/art43/).

---

### E. **Climate change: shifting winds and phenology (timing) mismatches**
**Phenology**
- Broad syntheses show that differential warming between wintering and breeding areas can produce migration timing mismatches with food peaks, and across many populations this correlates with declines (pattern differs between Nearctic and Palaearctic in one large analysis) [[69]](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1365-2656.2009.01610.x)[[68]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2781852/).

**Wind regimes and route feasibility**
- For wind-dependent migrants (e.g., soaring birds), models project that climate-driven changes in winds could reduce and even eliminate suitability of critical route segments (example: projected loss of a traditional honey-buzzard sea-crossing route section by late century) [[59]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rspb/article/284/1854/20170149/102112/Climate-change-alters-the-optimal-wind-dependent).

---

### F. **Habitat loss and fragmentation: loss of navigational “targets” and stopover bottlenecks**
Even if compasses and maps are intact, migrants must *find places to survive en route*.

- Stopovers are repeatedly chosen and are energetically critical; degradation increases migration costs and jeopardizes success, especially for inexperienced birds and near barriers where birds concentrate [[56]](https://research.fs.usda.gov/treesearch/download/22889.pdf).
- A major flyway example: **>65% loss** of Yellow Sea tidal mudflats has been linked to steep declines in shorebirds that rely most strongly on that stopover region (up to ~8% per year in some taxa; strongest declines in taxa with >50% reliance) [[63]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5399291/).

**Navigation connection**
Stopover loss increases the cost of errors: birds that drift, are displaced, or are forced down by bad weather have fewer safe refueling options, turning navigational imperfections into mortality.

---

### G. **Built infrastructure collisions beyond lighting: glass and power lines**
- Glass collisions are a major mortality source; small structures like **glass bus shelters** can also cause measurable fatalities, with collision risk rising with glass area and nearby lawn/green space [[66]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5453692/).
- Power lines in the U.S. are estimated to kill **12–64 million birds annually** (8–57M collisions; 0.9–11.6M electrocutions) [[31]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4081594/).

**Mitigation**
- Bird-safe glass treatments and architectural retrofits (patterns, films, screens, netting, external barriers) are widely recommended and can be inexpensive in some implementations [[57]](https://www.fws.gov/sites/default/files/documents/reducing-bird-collisions-with-buildings.pdf).
- Power line mortality is reducible via retrofits and bird-safe construction standards [[31]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4081594/).

---

### H. **Toxins/pesticides: delayed migration and altered departure decisions**
- Field-realistic dosing of migrating white-crowned sparrows with the neonicotinoid **imidacloprid** reduced feeding and fattening and delayed departure from stopover (median +3.5 days in high-dose treatment), directly altering migration timing and potentially increasing downstream risks [[62]](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw9419).

---

## 7) Synthesis: how birds achieve precision—and what most threatens it
**Precision in migratory navigation is emergent**: it comes from layering multiple compasses (magnetic, sun, stars, polarization), one or more map systems (often olfactory and/or magnetic intensity cues plus learned visual geography), and ongoing correction for winds and barriers.

- **Compass precision** is protected by redundancy (magnetic when skies are closed; stars on clear nights; sun by day; twilight for potential recalibration) but is vulnerable when multiple cues degrade simultaneously (e.g., overcast + geomagnetic storms; urban EM noise + skyglow) [[29]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306317120)[[27]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24805233/)[[23]](https://lightsoutheartland.org/images/articles/Burt_TREE_2023EcolandEvolrevlighteffectsonmigration2023.pdf).
- **Map precision** often improves with **experience**, enabling adults to correct displacements that juveniles cannot—yet in some taxa first-time migrants can show partial compensation, suggesting flexibility and learning during the first journey in at least some systems [[15]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11285401/)[[49]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-64230-x)[[44]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/199/1/49/7362/Genetic-Basis-Mode-of-Inheritance-and-Evolutionary).
- The strongest, well-quantified modern disturbances are **ALAN-driven disorientation/aggregation and collision amplification** [[24]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1708574114)[[25]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8214683/)[[26]](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2101666118), and **anthropogenic EM noise** disrupting the magnetic compass under realistic urban conditions [[27]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24805233/).
- At flyway scales, **stopover loss** can convert navigational challenges into population bottlenecks [[56]](https://research.fs.usda.gov/treesearch/download/22889.pdf)[[63]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5399291/), and **climate change** can reshape both the timing and the atmospheric “conveyor belts” migration depends on [[69]](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1365-2656.2009.01610.x)[[59]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rspb/article/284/1854/20170149/102112/Climate-change-alters-the-optimal-wind-dependent).
