## 1) Defining “the world’s wealthiest governments” (2024–2026): the investable-asset lens

When people say “wealthiest governments,” they often mean one (or a mix) of these *state-controlled investable pools*:

1) **Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs)** (savings funds, stabilization funds, strategic/development funds, etc.)  
2) **Central bank (or treasury) reserve portfolios** (foreign exchange reserves + related foreign-currency assets)  
3) **Public pension / retirement reserve funds** (national pension reserves, public employee systems, social security trust funds)  
4) **State holding companies / national investment corporations** (often strategic + domestic development objectives; sometimes SWF-adjacent)

Because these pools sit in different institutions (finance ministries, central banks, independent pension boards, state holding companies), “wealthiest” depends on **which pool(s) you include** and whether you consolidate by country. Public rankings are typically **fund-by-fund**, not fully consolidated national balance sheets.

## 2) Vehicles, mandates, and governance models: how “wealthy governments” organize investing

### A. Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs): the Santiago Principles governance baseline
The **Santiago Principles** (GAPP) define SWFs as special-purpose investment funds owned by the general government, created for macroeconomic purposes, with financial objectives, often investing in foreign assets [[20]](https://www.ifswf.org/sites/default/files/santiagoprinciples_0_0.pdf). They recognize SWFs are heterogeneous (stabilization, savings, development funds, pension reserve funds without explicit liabilities, etc.) [[20]](https://www.ifswf.org/sites/default/files/santiagoprinciples_0_0.pdf).  

The principles emphasize:  
- clarity of legal basis and objectives  
- separation of owner/governing body/management to facilitate operational independence  
- investment and risk management framework  
- transparency and accountability (voluntary; subject to local law) [[20]](https://www.ifswf.org/sites/default/files/santiagoprinciples_0_0.pdf)

The International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds (IFSWF) positions the Santiago Principles as globally accepted standards for governance/investment/risk management and notes members self-assess and publish adherence reviews (voluntary; subordinate to local law) [[19]](https://www.ifswf.org/santiago-principles).

### B. Central bank reserve managers: liquidity-first with governance and transparency norms
The IMF’s **Revised Guidelines for Foreign Exchange Reserve Management** describe reserve objectives that are primarily **policy and liquidity oriented**: confidence in monetary/exchange-rate policy, intervention capacity, external liquidity to absorb shocks, ability to meet external obligations, disaster/emergency buffers—while still aiming for “reasonable risk-adjusted returns” subject to liquidity and risk constraints [[21]](https://www.imf.org/external/np/mae/ferm/2014/revgudferm14.pdf).  

They emphasize:  
- clear responsibilities and legislative framework  
- separation of investment management and risk control  
- public disclosure of objectives, responsibilities, reserve data schedules  
- annual external audit and strong internal governance/audit [[21]](https://www.imf.org/external/np/mae/ferm/2014/revgudferm14.pdf)

The World Bank’s **Reserve Management Survey Report 2025** finds most reserve managers still use a **three-tier governance structure**: board (strategy/policy), investment committee (implementation), front/middle/back offices (execution and controls) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf). It also shows increasingly rich risk reporting (duration, VaR, CVaR, tracking error, etc.) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

The IMF’s **Safeguards Assessment—2025 Update** describes how IMF safeguards work to reduce risks in IMF lending, and notes persistent vulnerabilities often remain in central banks’ governance and controls, including in foreign reserves and gold management [[23]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/pp/2025/english/ppea2025029.pdf).

### C. Public pension / retirement reserve funds: fiduciary duty + asset/liability framing
Public pension funds typically have **explicit beneficiaries and liabilities**, which leads to:
- governance via boards with fiduciary duties
- long horizon, but with liquidity needs to pay benefits
- asset-liability management and strategic asset allocation as a central governance act

Examples from the provided sources:
- **Japan GPIF**: invests “for the sole benefit” of insureds, aims to secure required returns with minimal risk over long term, ensures liquidity; governance via Board of Governors (President + external experts) deciding key policy (including Policy Asset Mix) and overseeing execution [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  
- **CPP Investments (Canada)**: independent Crown corporation designed to invest professionally and independently of government; board oversight of risk appetite and major decisions [[28]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/CPP-Investments-F2024-Annual-Report.pdf).  
- **CalSTRS (California)**: Teachers’ Retirement Board has “sole and exclusive fiduciary responsibility,” prudent person/expert standard, strong delegation framework; board sets policy allocation and rebalancing ranges and authorizes derivatives for exposure/rebalancing efficiency [[29]](https://www.calstrs.com/files/96d83d6f4/INV+012024+Item+05.03+-+Investment+Policy+Statement+%E2%80%93+Clean+Version+-+Attachment+3.pdf).  
- **MassPRIM (Massachusetts PRIT)**: statutory mission to address unfunded liabilities; board sets policy benchmarks, manager concentration limits, rebalancing rules, hedge fund role, etc. [[30]](https://www.mapension.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/PRIM-Investment-Policy-Statement-02152024.pdf).

### D. Hybrid / state holding company models (strategic + commercial)
Some very large government investors combine commercial investing with state-development goals. Example:  
- **Mubadala (Abu Dhabi)** is a state investor with disclosed AUM and portfolio mix; it also raises capital in markets and reports gearing ratios, reflecting a corporate-like balance sheet model [[37]](https://annual2024.mubadala.com/2024-financial-performance) [[38]](https://www.mubadala.com/en/news/mubadala-investment-company-reports-2024-financial-results).  
- **ADQ** appears as a major state holding company with large consolidated assets and economic diversification focus, though its financial statements (in the captured extract) do not present SWF-style asset allocation tables [[39]](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/63159b6a1b1c7641eaae3d7f/67fea14a69e031c1e317712a_ADQ%20Group%20Consolidated%20YE%202024_Signed.pdf).

## 3) Asset allocation and exposures of major government investors (what they actually hold)

A key reality: **disclosure differs dramatically** by institution and jurisdiction. Norway GPFG, Japan GPIF, Singapore GIC and Temasek are unusually informative in public materials; other major pools (e.g., some Gulf SWFs, some Chinese entities) disclose much less in the extracts available here.

### A. Norway: GPFG (NBIM) — the most transparent mega-SWF
**End-2024 asset allocation (market value weights):**  
- Equities **71.4%**  
- Fixed income **26.6%**  
- Unlisted real estate **1.8%**  
- Unlisted renewable energy infrastructure **0.1%** [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf)

**2024 asset-class returns:** equities **18.2%**, fixed income **1.3%**, unlisted real estate **-0.6%**, renewables infra **-9.8%** [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  

**Fixed income composition (within fixed income, end-2024):** government bonds **60.2%** of FI; corporate **24.8%**; government-related **9.4%**; inflation-linked **5.8%**; securitised **5.8%** (note: cash/derivatives not included in those shares) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  

**Currency exposures inside fixed income (shares of FI by currency, end-2024):** USD **52.4%**, EUR **27.6%**, JPY **5.3%**, CAD **5.3%**, GBP **4.6%**, SGD **4.2%**, etc. [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  

**Real estate:** total real estate **~722bn NOK** end-2024 (unlisted **~363.6bn**, listed **~358.5bn**) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf). Unlisted real estate is concentrated in major cities (Paris, London, Boston, NY, etc.) and logistics (primarily US) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  

**Renewable infrastructure:** unlisted infrastructure value **~25.3bn NOK** end-2024; investments include solar/wind in Iberia, offshore wind in the UK, and fund commitment to Copenhagen Infrastructure V [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).

**Constraints:** upper limit **7%** of fund value in unlisted real estate; renewable infrastructure cap exists in NBIM key-figures tables (also with Ministry limits) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf) [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/).

### B. Japan: GPIF — policy allocation is explicit; alternatives capped
GPIF invests primarily across four building blocks. For the **5th Medium-Term Objectives Period (from 1 April 2025)**, GPIF keeps targets unchanged:  
- Domestic bonds **25%**  
- Foreign bonds **25%**  
- Domestic equities **25%**  
- Foreign equities **25%** [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf)

Deviation limits are tightened vs the prior period (e.g., domestic equities ±6%, foreign equities ±6%; global equity/bond bands ±9%) [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf).  

**Alternatives** (infrastructure/private equity/real estate, etc.) are *not separate asset classes* but managed within the four assets by risk-return characteristics, with an **upper limit of 5%** of the total portfolio [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf).  

Benchmark changes also embed policy: for example, GPIF’s foreign bond benchmark excludes China; foreign equity benchmark excludes China A-shares and includes tax-adjustment considerations [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf).

### C. Singapore: GIC — published high-level asset mix + reference portfolio concept
GIC discloses asset mix and a “Reference Portfolio” risk anchor.

**Asset mix (as at 31 March):**  
- 2025: Equities **51%**, Fixed Income **26%**, Real Assets **23%**  
- 2024: Equities **46%**, Fixed Income **32%**, Real Assets **22%** [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf)

GIC’s **Reference Portfolio** is **65% global equities / 35% global bonds** (stated as a risk reference, not a performance benchmark) [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  

**Geographic exposure monitoring view (31 March 2025):** Americas **44%**, EMEA **20%**, Asia Pacific **28%**, Global **8%** [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  

GIC does not present detailed currency/duration/leverage constraints in the captured excerpt, but it does report volatility and returns and discusses top-down + bottom-up construction [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).

### D. Singapore: Temasek — concentrated, equity-like state holding company with detailed risk language
Temasek reports a **Net Portfolio Value (NPV)** and provides multiple portfolio breakdowns.

**NPV:**  
- As at **31 March 2024**: **S$389B** (mark-to-market uplift would take it to **S$420B**) [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf) [[66]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/performance-and-portfolio.html)  
- As at **31 March 2025**: **S$434B** (mark-to-market uplift to **S$469B**) [[42]](https://www.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2025-full-version.pdf)

**Portfolio segmentation (2025):**  
- Singapore-based Temasek Portfolio Companies **41%**  
- Global Direct Investments **36%**

**2024 exposures (examples):**

- Geography (HQ, 2024): Singapore **53%**, China **13%**, India **5%**, Americas **17%**, EMEA **10%** [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf) [[66]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/performance-and-portfolio.html).

### E. Abu Dhabi / UAE: multiple large vehicles with different disclosure styles
**Mubadala** (more “corporate balance sheet” style) discloses:  
- **AUM 2024:** **AED 1,212B (~US$330B)** [[37]](https://annual2024.mubadala.com/2024-financial-performance)  
- **Portfolio by asset class (2024):** Private **40%**, Public **23%**, Real Estate & Infrastructure **17%**, Alternatives **15%**, Credit **5%** [[37]](https://annual2024.mubadala.com/2024-financial-performance)

## 4) How wealthy governments implement strategy in practice (benchmarks, rebalancing, risk, leverage/derivatives, internal/external, ESG)

### A. Benchmarking models: three common patterns

**1) “Public benchmark + limited tracking error” (Norway GPFG)**  
- Ministry sets benchmark; NBIM manages with **expected relative volatility (tracking error) limit 1.25%** [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf) [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/).  
- NBIM decomposes active results into “market exposure,” “security selection,” and “fund allocation” [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  

**2) “Policy portfolio weights (SAA) + deviation bands + futures for rebalancing” (Japan GPIF)**  
- GPIF’s policy asset mix is explicit (25/25/25/25) and deviation limits are set and tightened in 2025 policy mix [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf).  
- GPIF reports using **stock index futures and bond futures** to rebalance and manage deviations efficiently [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  

**3) “Reference portfolio defines risk; actual portfolio differs structurally” (GIC; CPP)**  
- GIC: Reference Portfolio 65/35 global equity/bonds defines risk tolerance; actual portfolio is a diversified multi-asset portfolio (Equities/Fixed Income/Real Assets) with long-horizon evaluation (rolling 20-year real returns) [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  
- CPP: reference portfolios (bond + global equity mix) represent market risk target; actual portfolio is much more diversified and private-market heavy; performance is evaluated vs reference portfolios in value-added terms [[55]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/CPP-Investments-F2024-News-Release.pdf) [[62]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/newsroom/cpp-investments-net-assets-total-714-4-billion-at-2025-fiscal-year-end/).

### B. Rebalancing and liquidity management

- **GPIF**: explicitly highlights “timely and appropriate” rebalancing to stay consistent with policy asset mix; used futures for rebalancing in FY2024 [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  
- **CalSTRS**: policy ranges and explicit delegation for rebalancing; CIO authorized to rebalance and may use derivatives to maintain exposure and reduce transaction costs [[29]](https://www.calstrs.com/files/96d83d6f4/INV+012024+Item+05.03+-+Investment+Policy+Statement+%E2%80%93+Clean+Version+-+Attachment+3.pdf).  
- **Temasek**: focuses on balance-sheet resilience; states it maintains high liquidity, manages leverage within a board-set debt limit, and selectively hedges FX tied to nearer-term cash flows/divestments [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf).  
- **Invesco sovereign study** (survey-based): highlights that private-market growth has elevated liquidity frameworks, with many sovereign institutions implementing formalized liquidity caps and monitoring systems [[76]](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2025/july/invesco-global-sovereign-asset-management-study-2025.pdf). (This is a secondary, industry survey source, but it aligns with what large investors discuss operationally.)

### C. Risk frameworks: from tracking error to stress testing and CVaR

**NBIM (Norway GPFG): unusually explicit risk limits and stress testing**  
NBIM discloses a detailed limits framework including:  
- expected relative volatility limit and current readings [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)  
- liquidity requirements (e.g., minimum % in certain major treasury markets) [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)  
- leverage caps (e.g., max 5% of equity + fixed income investments; reported 0.5 at 30.06.2025) [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)  
- securities lending/borrowing caps, counterparty limits, option issuance limits, CFD exposure limits [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)  
- unlisted real estate: debt ratio max 25% (reported 8.9), vacancy and development limits, country/sector bands [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)  
- unlisted renewables: debt ratio limits and construction-phase limits [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/)

NBIM’s **stress testing** explicitly models historical and hypothetical scenarios and discusses themes including AI corrections, fragmentation, debt crises, and extreme weather; it reports simulated drawdowns for historical scenarios (e.g., GFC -29.8% simulation, COVID -13.3%) and forward-looking scenario impacts where “Fragmented world” produces the largest drawdown in its examples [[50]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/1c26bc90eb274356a60fd77ae5a5bf49/gpfg_stresstesting-2025.pdf).  

**Reserve managers:** World Bank survey shows increasing adoption of sophisticated measures; **CVaR surpassing VaR** as a reported metric is a notable shift [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

### D. Active vs passive; internal vs external management; cost control

- **NBIM** uses both internal and external managers; decomposes relative return contribution; emphasizes cost efficiency and reports very low costs (2024 management cost 0.04% AUM) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  
- **GPIF** uses both passive and active management to achieve benchmark returns and seek additional opportunities; discloses very low external manager/custodian fee ratio (~0.01%) [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  
- **CPP Investments** discloses detailed cost categories (operating expenses, management fees, performance fees, transaction costs) and reports operating expense ratio 26.1 bps in FY2025 [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf) [[62]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/newsroom/cpp-investments-net-assets-total-714-4-billion-at-2025-fiscal-year-end/).  
- **Temasek** emphasizes intrinsic value, a risk-adjusted cost of capital framework (RACOC) for capital allocation, and board risk appetite statements rather than a single public benchmark [[54]](https://www.temasek.com.sg/content/dam/temasek-corporate/our-financials/investor-library/annual-review/en-tr-thumbnail-and-pdf/Temasek-Review-2024-Highlights.pdf) [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf).

### E. ESG, climate, stewardship, and voting
- **Santiago Principles** include governance and transparency norms and encourage financial risk/return driven investing with sound controls, but do not prescribe ESG specifics [[20]](https://www.ifswf.org/sites/default/files/santiagoprinciples_0_0.pdf).  
- **GPIF** explicitly embeds sustainability: believes sustainable growth and ESG/impact matter for long-term returns; it adopted “Asset Owner Principles” (government-led) in 2024 and created a “Sustainability Investment Policy” in March 2025 [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  
- **CPP Investments** integrated its “Report on Sustainable Investing” into the FY2025 annual report, and includes extensive cautionary language about evolving standards and third-party data limitations [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- **Temasek** describes ESG integration in due diligence and post-investment engagement and views voting/engagement as levers of long-term value creation [[54]](https://www.temasek.com.sg/content/dam/temasek-corporate/our-financials/investor-library/annual-review/en-tr-thumbnail-and-pdf/Temasek-Review-2024-Highlights.pdf).  
- **Reserve managers**: the World Bank survey shows sustainability integration rising sharply (7% in 2021 to 35% in 2025) among central banks, though with constraints vs traditional safety/liquidity mandates [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

## 5) Performance and risk outcomes (recent years; differences by mandate)

### A. Norway GPFG (NBIM): high equity beta + disciplined active risk
- **2024 return:** **13.1%** [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf)  
- **2022 drawdown year:** **-14.1%** [[65]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/returns/)  
- **2023:** **16.1%** [[65]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/returns/)  
- **1H 2025:** **5.7%** [[65]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/returns/)  
- **Long-run average (1998–2024):** **6.3%** average annual return; **4.1%** net real return after inflation and management costs [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  
- **Risk stats (as at 30 June 2025):** standard deviation ~9.80% (10y), tracking error ~0.39% (10y) [[65]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/returns/).  
- **Costs:** 2024 management cost **0.04% of AUM**; detailed cost attribution shows external mandates are far more expensive on an AUM-basis than internal [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf).  
- **Key drivers:** equity markets and US tech concentration were dominant in 2024; NOK weakness boosted local-currency valuation; high oil prices increased inflows [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/).  
- **Real assets sensitivity to rates:** renewables infra -9.8% in 2024 driven “in particular” by higher cost of capital [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/).

This profile is typical of a **long-horizon savings SWF** with large equity exposure: high expected return, meaningful drawdowns in crisis years, and a strong focus on cost and governance.

### B. Japan GPIF: policy mix discipline and modest active deviations
- **FY2024 return:** **0.71%** (annual rate), with excess return vs compound benchmark **+0.09%** [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf).  
- GPIF highlights futures-based rebalancing during volatile bond and equity markets and tightening deviation limits for the next medium-term period [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf) [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf).  
This resembles a **giant pension reserve fund**: strategic allocation dominates; active risk is tightly controlled; liquidity and beneficiary focus is explicit.

### C. Singapore GIC: long-horizon real-return metric and volatility management
- **20-year (to 31 Mar 2025):** 5.7% nominal USD, **3.8% real** [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  
- **Volatility (20y):** **8.7%** (standard deviation of monthly returns) [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  
- Reference portfolio (65/35) has higher volatility; GIC notes its portfolio volatility is consistently lower, attributing this to diversified composition and pre-emptive risk reductions [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf).  
This is characteristic of a **state savings investor** optimizing purchasing power across generations, emphasizing real returns and resilience.

### D. Temasek: equity-like return profile; heavy unlisted exposure; explicit stress thinking
- **NPV 2024:** S$389B; **1-year TSR 1.60%**, 10-year TSR 6%, 20-year TSR 7% [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf) [[63]](https://www.temasek.com.sg/en/news-and-resources/news-room/news/2024/temasek-review-2024-389-billion-net-portfolio-value).  
- **NPV 2025:** S$434B; **1-year TSR 11.8%**, 10-year TSR 5%, 20-year TSR 7% [[42]](https://www.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2025-full-version.pdf).  
- Reports Monte Carlo range: “five-in-six chance” one-year forward returns between **-10% and +15%** for current mix; historic annual returns ranged **-30% to +43%** over past 20 years [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf).  
Temasek looks like a **state-owned concentrated global equity investor / holding company**: higher idiosyncratic risk, large unlisted book, strong balance sheet governance focus.

### E. Canada CPP Investments: diversified public + private; value-added vs reference portfolio can swing
- **FY2025 net return:** **9.3%**, net assets **$714.4B** [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- FY2025 costs: operating expenses, management fees, performance fees, transaction costs disclosed in detail; operating expense ratio **26.1 bps** [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf) [[62]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/newsroom/cpp-investments-net-assets-total-714-4-billion-at-2025-fiscal-year-end/).  
- CPP emphasizes that in strong public equity rallies (e.g., tech-led), the simpler reference portfolios can outperform in a given year, while CPP’s diversified/private-heavy structure aims to reduce downside and deliver more consistent long-term performance [[62]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/newsroom/cpp-investments-net-assets-total-714-4-billion-at-2025-fiscal-year-end/) [[55]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/CPP-Investments-F2024-News-Release.pdf).  
This matches a **large public pension investor** managing through cycles, with governance attention to geopolitics, AI, climate, and regulation [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).

In the provided materials, we have less direct return reporting for reserve managers. Instead, the trend evidence is strong:
- World Bank survey indicates rebalancing from short-term instruments into higher-yielding fixed income and increasing use of CVaR and richer risk reporting [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

## 6) 2024–2026 trends reshaping how wealthy governments invest (with concrete examples)

### Trend 1: “Higher for longer” rates change the role of bonds—and pressure real assets
- **Reserve managers**: World Bank survey reports a move away from short-term instruments toward higher-yielding fixed income securities (rebalancing to capture yield) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).  
- **Sovereign investors**: Invesco’s sovereign study reports renewed interest in fixed income, with many SWFs planning to increase fixed income exposure and treating it as both yield and liquidity buffer [[76]](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2025/july/invesco-global-sovereign-asset-management-study-2025.pdf).  
- **Norway GPFG**: renewables infra negative return driven by higher cost of capital illustrates how higher rates pressure discounted long-duration assets [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/).

### Trend 2: Inflation hedging + diversification pushes gold higher
- IMF analysis ties sanctions risk and uncertainty to shifts toward gold, describing gold as free of sanctions risk if stored domestically [[72]](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2024/06/11/dollar-dominance-in-the-international-reserve-system-an-update).  
- IMF leadership speech reiterates gold as politically neutral and an inflation hedge; notes rising gold share in some “China bloc” reserve portfolios and gives China-specific examples [[73]](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2024/05/07/sp-geopolitics-impact-global-trade-and-dollar-gita-gopinath).  
- World Gold Council survey (2025) reports central banks buying >1,000 tonnes per year for three consecutive years; 95% expect global gold reserves to rise; motivations include crisis performance, diversification, inflation hedging [[74]](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/central-bank-gold-reserves-survey-2025).  
- World Bank survey says gold’s role is growing mainly for diversification, with geopolitical considerations also cited [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

### Trend 3: Gradual reserve currency diversification away from USD (but not collapse)
- IMF (2024) finds the USD share of allocated reserves has gradually declined over two decades, with diversification into “nontraditional reserve currencies” (AUD, CAD, KRW, SGD, Nordic currencies, RMB, etc.) rather than the traditional “big four” [[72]](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2024/06/11/dollar-dominance-in-the-international-reserve-system-an-update).  
- IMF also states there is no statistical evidence of an *accelerating* decline despite sanctions narratives, though it notes non-reporting countries could bias measurement at the margin [[72]](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2024/06/11/dollar-dominance-in-the-international-reserve-system-an-update).  
- World Gold Council survey suggests many reserve managers expect moderately lower USD shares over five years and higher shares for other currencies and gold [[74]](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/central-bank-gold-reserves-survey-2025).  
- World Bank survey still finds USD and EUR dominate average allocations (USD 64%, EUR 24% across respondents), underscoring the slow-moving nature of reserve shifts [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

### Trend 4: Geopolitical fragmentation becomes an explicit investment and risk variable
- IMF speech (May 2024) documents sharp increases in trade restrictions and describes FDI and trade re-routing along geopolitical lines; it also links fragmentation to currency and reserve considerations [[73]](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2024/05/07/sp-geopolitics-impact-global-trade-and-dollar-gita-gopinath).  
- CPP Investments’ board explicitly focuses on geopolitical dynamics, and on how trade patterns, tensions and regulation may affect the fund [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- NBIM’s scenario work includes “fragmented world” stress testing and reports large potential drawdowns in extreme scenarios [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/) [[50]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/1c26bc90eb274356a60fd77ae5a5bf49/gpfg_stresstesting-2025.pdf).

### Trend 5: Private markets keep growing—especially private credit
- Invesco sovereign study: private credit adoption among SWFs rises to 73%, with many increasing allocations and building origination/structuring capabilities; SWFs cite floating-rate protection and bespoke structuring [[76]](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2025/july/invesco-global-sovereign-asset-management-study-2025.pdf).  
- ADIA explicitly discusses a “dynamic approach to private credit” within its target exposure [[14]](https://www.adia.ae/en/pr/2024/pdf/adia-annual-review-2024_final.pdf).  
- EY report expects SWF AUM growth partly driven by increased allocations to private markets; notes infrastructure and private market activity among MENA SWFs [[77]](https://www.ey.com/content/dam/ey-unified-site/ey-com/en-ae/insights/wealth-asset-management/documents/ey-mena-swf-report-2024-05-2025.pdf).  
- IFSWF/State Street publication notes investors sought private markets for stability and long-horizon advantages in 2024 conditions [[75]](https://ifswf.org/navigating-market-divergence-and-new-narratives-evidence-long-term-investors).

### Trend 6: Tech/AI concentration risk is recognized and managed explicitly
- NBIM states benchmark concentration has risen, with tech among the largest contributors to returns; it notes its underweight to the largest US tech names reduced relative return in 2024 (a deliberate risk profile choice) [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/).  
- NBIM includes “repricing of AI companies” as a stress test theme and reports large drawdowns in extreme scenario sets [[64]](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/) [[50]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/1c26bc90eb274356a60fd77ae5a5bf49/gpfg_stresstesting-2025.pdf).  
- CPP board education agenda includes generative AI as a governance topic [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- World Bank survey: only 12% of reserve managers use AI/ML tools in reserve management frameworks, indicating slow operational adoption in official reserves despite broader AI narratives [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

### Trend 7: Climate transition shifts from “policy” to “capital platforms” and new vehicles
A concrete, dated example of a wealthy-government-backed climate capital platform is **ALTÉRRA**:
- UAE-backed: initial UAE commitment **$30B**; aims to mobilize **$250B by 2030**; dual-arm structure (Acceleration and Transformation funds) [[80]](https://www.alterra.ae/) [[81]](https://www.bbva.com/en/sustainability/bbva-and-alterra-forge-strategic-partnership-backed-by-250-million-commitment-to-new-climate-fund/).  
- Ongoing commitments and partnerships (news feed + announcements) include co-investments and commitments to climate platforms and funds [[80]](https://www.alterra.ae/).  
- In 2026, BBVA and ALTÉRRA announced plans for a **$1.2B** climate co-investment vehicle (BBVA proposed strategic LP $250M; subject to approvals), with a diversified climate-aligned strategy across infrastructure, private equity and private credit and domicile in ADGM [[81]](https://www.bbva.com/en/sustainability/bbva-and-alterra-forge-strategic-partnership-backed-by-250-million-commitment-to-new-climate-fund/).

### Trend 8: Sustainability disclosure regimes and standards reshape investor reporting and investee data
- OECD Factbook 2025 documents adoption timelines for ISSB-aligned disclosure regimes and phased implementation in multiple jurisdictions (EU CSRD phases; Hong Kong climate disclosures based on IFRS S2; Japan’s SSBJ standards aligned with ISSB with phased mandatory adoption from FY starting April 2026 for very large Prime Market companies; Mexico ISSB-aligned sustainability reports from 2026) [[78]](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-corporate-governance-factbook-2025_f4f43735-en/full-report/corporate-sustainability_5382e187.html).  
- CPP Investments explicitly flags that sustainability standards and third-party data are evolving and may require restatement or changes to objectives/disclosures [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- Reserve managers: the share integrating sustainability into policies rose to 35% in 2025 in the World Bank survey [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf).

## 7) What this implies: “how the wealthiest governments invest” in one integrated picture

### A. The common architecture
Across SWFs, pensions, and reserve managers, the biggest government investors typically converge on:
- **A strategic policy portfolio** (benchmark index, policy asset mix, or reference portfolio) anchoring risk [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf) [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf) [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf) [[55]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/CPP-Investments-F2024-News-Release.pdf)  
- **Controlled active risk** (tracking error constraints, deviation bands, or governance risk appetite statements) [[49]](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/risk-management/) [[51]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/topics/Adoption_of_New_Policy_Portfolio_details_2_en.pdf) [[26]](https://tr24.temasekreview.com.sg/downloads/Temasek-Review-2024-full-version.pdf)  
- **Growing private market sleeves** (real estate, infrastructure, private equity, private credit), often justified by long horizon and diversification [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf) [[13]](https://www.gic.com.sg/uploads/2025/07/GIC_AR_2024-25_PRINT.pdf) [[76]](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2025/july/invesco-global-sovereign-asset-management-study-2025.pdf)  
- **Institutionalized risk measurement** (stress testing, VaR/CVaR, scenario analysis, liquidity monitoring) [[50]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/1c26bc90eb274356a60fd77ae5a5bf49/gpfg_stresstesting-2025.pdf) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf)  
- **Increasing formalization of sustainability/climate policies and reporting**, with uneven implementation due to mandate constraints [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf) [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf)

### B. The key differences by mandate
- **Reserve portfolios**: prioritize liquidity/safety; diversification is cautious; gold and nontraditional currencies grow slowly; sustainability integration is rising but constrained [[21]](https://www.imf.org/external/np/mae/ferm/2014/revgudferm14.pdf) [[22]](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099648211072524788/pdf/IDU-f1aeb421-97fb-4a82-a223-0ecbbe0bdbb8.pdf) [[72]](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2024/06/11/dollar-dominance-in-the-international-reserve-system-an-update) [[74]](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/central-bank-gold-reserves-survey-2025).  
- **Long-horizon SWFs (savings)**: can hold higher equity and illiquidity; large scenario and tail risk is accepted and governed; costs and transparency vary widely (Norway is the gold standard) [[24]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/490f9f062cfc4694b12c45f4d04ab0a5/annual_report_2024.pdf) [[50]](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/1c26bc90eb274356a60fd77ae5a5bf49/gpfg_stresstesting-2025.pdf) [[20]](https://www.ifswf.org/sites/default/files/santiagoprinciples_0_0.pdf).  
- **Public pensions**: explicit fiduciary duty + liabilities; policy mix and rebalancing discipline are central; cost and governance reporting tends to be detailed, especially in common-law jurisdictions [[11]](https://www.gpif.go.jp/en/performance/annual_report_fiscal_year_2024.pdf) [[29]](https://www.calstrs.com/files/96d83d6f4/INV+012024+Item+05.03+-+Investment+Policy+Statement+%E2%80%93+Clean+Version+-+Attachment+3.pdf) [[12]](https://www.cppinvestments.com/wp-content/uploads/attachments/CPP-Investments-F2025-Annual-Report-English.pdf).  
- **State holding companies / strategic vehicles**: may mix national development objectives with commercial returns; often have balance sheet leverage and capital markets activity (e.g., Mubadala gearing and issuance) [[38]](https://www.mubadala.com/en/news/mubadala-investment-company-reports-2024-financial-results).

## 8) Practical “next steps” if you’re continuing this research
If your goal is a comparative dataset, the biggest gap is **inconsistent disclosure**. A robust workflow is:

1) Pick a consolidation approach: **country-consolidated** vs **fund-by-fund**.  
2) Standardize categories: public equity, fixed income, cash, real estate, infrastructure, PE/VC, private credit, hedge funds, gold/commodities.  
3) Use primary reports where possible (annual reports/financial statements/mandates) and treat industry rankings as estimates.  
4) Track governance/risk frameworks explicitly (tracking error / deviation bands / reference portfolio; stress testing; liquidity frameworks).  
5) Add a “transparency score” (e.g., Santiago Principles self-assessments for SWFs, public pension statutes/policies, reserve manager disclosure schedule).
