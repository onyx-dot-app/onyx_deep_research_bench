ANSWER:
## 1) Scope, definitions, and how to read this report

**Elderly definition (Japan):** I use three age bands that are commonly used in Japan’s official statistics and business planning:

- **65+** (“elderly” / 高齢者)
- **75+** (“late-stage elderly” / 後期高齢者に近い実務的バンド)
- **85+** (“old-old”, a key driver of care needs and household miniaturization)

**Population projection source:** IPSS (National Institute of Population and Social Security Research) **Population Projections for Japan (2023 revision)**, *medium fertility, medium mortality*, total population **including foreigners**, as-of **October 1** each year [[2]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-1.xlsx)[[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx).

**Consumption / market sizing approach (important):**
- Your question asks “consumption potential across clothing, food, housing, transportation” and a “market size analysis report.”
- Japan’s best *public, consistently available* consumption-by-age data is the **Family Income and Expenditure Survey (FIES)**, which is **household-based**.
- Therefore, the core market model in this report sizes “elderly consumption potential” as:  
  **(Projected number of elderly-headed households) × (per-household consumption by category)**  
  using FIES per-household spending for **households with household head aged 65+** [[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls) and IPSS projections for **household head aged 65+ households** [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).

This is **not identical** to “all consumption by all individuals aged 65+” because:
- Some people aged 65+ live in households whose head is <65 (e.g., multigenerational households).
- Some 65+ head households include non-elderly members (children, etc.).  
Still, this approach is widely used for market sizing because it aligns naturally to purchasing units (households) and to housing/utility/transport expenditure measurement.

**Money units:** FIES is in **nominal yen** (survey year yen). For 2050 projections I provide:
- **Real (constant purchasing power)** projections (expressed in “2023 yen” terms for simplicity), and
- **Nominal 2050 yen** scenarios using official long-run inflation assumption sets used in Japan’s pension actuarial framework: **2.0% / 0.8% / 0.4%** long-run inflation cases [[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf). (These are *assumptions*, not forecasts.)

## 2) How many elderly people will there be in Japan (2020–2050)?

### 2.1 Headline: 65+ remains ~39 million through mid-century, but “older-elderly” grows

Under IPSS 2023 (medium scenario), Japan’s **65+ population** stays around the **high 30 millions**, peaking in the 2040s and still ~38.9 million in 2050 [[2]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-1.xlsx). The more important structural change is **ageing within the elderly**: 75+ and especially 85+ become much larger shares of the elderly pool (driving different consumption mix).

### 2.2 Elderly population counts (IPSS 2023 medium) — 65+, 75+, 85+

All figures below are **millions of people**, as-of Oct 1.

- **75+ and 85+** are constructed from IPSS Table 1-9A by summing the published 5-year age groups (75–79…100+) and (85–89…100+) [[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx). (This matches Statistics Bureau 75+ reference values where available [[6]](https://www.stat.go.jp/data/topics/pdf/topics146.pdf).)

**Key implications for markets:**
- The “addressable senior market” is not just “65+”; it becomes increasingly a **75+/85+ market**, with very different needs (mobility support, home adaptation, convenience food, assisted living, etc.).
- Even if 65+ total flattens, **product/service mix shifts strongly** because the internal age composition changes.

## 3) Elderly “consumption potential” — what we know from official household spending data

### 3.1 Current consumption level for elderly-headed households (baseline basket)

The **FIES “Households with the aged”** table provides per-month spending for **household head aged 65+** households (average household size **1.86 persons**) [[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls). For 2023:

- **Total consumption expenditure:** **¥218,623 / month / household** [[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls)  
- Category spending (per month / household, 65+ head):
  - **Food:** **¥63,876** [[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls)
  - **Clothing & footwear:** **¥5,110** [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
  - **Housing (narrow, excludes utilities):** **¥14,817** [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
  - **Fuel, light & water (utilities):** **¥20,534** [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
  - **Transportation & communication:** **¥27,041** [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)  
    - of which **Public transportation:** ¥2,984; **Private transportation:** ¥15,543; **Communication:** ¥8,514 [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)

These numbers are a *practical “wallet allocation” snapshot* of elderly-headed households that can be scaled to a market.

### 3.2 Differences by elderly life stage (60–69 vs 70+) suggest mix/level shifts as Japan ages further

In the same FIES 2023 age-by-household-head table [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls):
- Total monthly consumption is **¥266,583** (head 60–69) vs **¥207,510** (head 70+) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).
- Clothing & footwear: **¥7,333** (60–69) vs **¥4,659** (70+) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).
- Transportation & communication: **¥40,667** (60–69) vs **¥24,008** (70+) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).
- Food is high in both, but lower at 70+: **¥72,790** (60–69) vs **¥61,591** (70+) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).

So, as the population shifts toward **older elderly**, **per-household total consumption and discretionary categories tend to decline**, while necessity and convenience-oriented categories become more central.

### 3.3 Willingness-to-spend: dissaving is common among non-working elderly; assets fund consumption gaps

Evidence from analysis using official household survey data shows that many retired elderly households **consume more than disposable income** and finance the gap by drawing down assets:

- Retired-couple households show **propensity to consume >100%** (example 124.2%) and a **negative saving rate** (example -24.2%) in the cited structure, implying dissaving [[30]](https://www.nli-research.co.jp/files/topics/51097_ext_18_en_0.pdf).
- Dissaving is largely funded by drawdown of financial assets (especially deposits) rather than borrowing [[30]](https://www.nli-research.co.jp/files/topics/51097_ext_18_en_0.pdf).
- Average net financial wealth (in the cited study) suggests many households *can* sustain dissaving for long periods on average, though disparities are large [[30]](https://www.nli-research.co.jp/files/topics/51097_ext_18_en_0.pdf).

**Market implication:** demand is not constrained only by current pension cashflow; it is also shaped by **asset decumulation behavior**, cohort wealth, and inflation/pension indexation dynamics.

## 4) Household structure through 2050 (critical for housing, food, and last-mile services)

Because the unit of purchase is often the household, Japan’s shift toward elderly living alone materially changes category demand (meal solutions, smaller-package goods, “aging-in-place” modifications, mobility support).

### 4.1 How many elderly-headed households will Japan have?

IPSS **Household Projections for Japan (2024 projection)** provide the future number of households by head’s age, based on 2020 Census and consistent population projections [[34]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_20240412.pdf)[[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).

**Households with head aged 65+ (millions):**
- 2020: **20.97m**
- 2030: **22.40m**
- 2040: **24.12m**
- 2050: **24.04m** [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)

So, even with population decline, the **number of elderly-headed households rises ~15%** from 2020 to 2050.

### 4.2 Elderly living alone increases strongly (a major demand shifter)

Within 65+ head households, **single-person households** grow sharply:

- 2020: **7.38m**
- 2030: **8.87m**
- 2040: **10.41m**
- 2050: **10.84m** [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)

By 2050, **45.1%** of 65+ head households are single-person households (up from 35.2% in 2020) [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx). IPSS also summarizes that the **living-alone rate among the 65+ population** rises markedly, especially for men (men 16.4%→26.1%; women 23.6%→29.3% from 2020 to 2050) [[37]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_PR.pdf).

**Market implication:** more single elderly means:
- More **small-portion food**, **prepared meals**, and **delivery**.
- More spending on **home safety**, **minor repairs**, and **support services** rather than large “family” purchases.
- Greater **mobility constraints** and demand for accessible transport alternatives.

### 5.4 Nominal 2050 market size ranges (inflation scenarios)

To convert the **2050 real (2023-yen)** figures into **2050 nominal yen**, you need an inflation assumption. Official long-run inflation assumptions used in Japan’s pension actuarial scenario setting include **2.0%**, **0.8%**, and **0.4%** long-run inflation rates depending on macro scenario [[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf). As a simple translation, applying constant annual inflation from 2023→2050 (~27 years) yields approximate multipliers:

- **0.4%**: ×**1.11**
- **0.8%**: ×**1.24**
- **2.0%**: ×**1.73**  
(These are illustrative multipliers for nominalization; actual inflation will vary year by year.)

Applying these multipliers to the **2050** real market sizes above gives approximate **2050 nominal** ranges:

- **Food (2050):** 18.43T × (1.11 to 1.73) ≈ **¥20.5T to ¥31.9T**
- **Transport & communication (2050):** 7.80T → **¥8.7T to ¥13.5T**
- **Utilities (2050):** 5.92T → **¥6.6T to ¥10.2T**
- **Housing (narrow, 2050):** 4.27T → **¥4.7T to ¥7.4T**
- **Clothing (2050):** 1.47T → **¥1.6T to ¥2.5T**  
Inflation framework references: [[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf); baseline spending & household counts: [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)[[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls)[[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).

## 6) Expected changes in consumption habits through 2050 (and what it does to market size)

The constant-real-spend scenario above is useful, but incomplete. The big drivers that can change per-household spend and category mix are:

1) **Age composition shift inside the elderly pool (65→75→85+)**  
2) **Household miniaturization / living alone** (rising strongly) [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)[[37]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_PR.pdf)  
3) **Budget pressure from inflation and essentials** (e.g., food price index rose strongly in recent years; CPI all-items 2025 annual average is 111.9 vs 2020=100) [[50]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/cpi/158c.html)  
4) **Pension and work/retirement changes** (replacement-rate framework, labor participation scenarios, coverage expansions) [[27]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/001270476.pdf)[[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf)  
5) **Substitution toward convenience services** (meal delivery, etc.)

Below is how these interact with your four categories.

## 7) Category-by-category consumption potential: what grows, what shifts, what is at risk

### 7.1 Clothing (apparel & footwear)

**Current reality (FIES):** elderly-headed households spend relatively little:
- **¥5,110/month** for 65+ head households in 2023 [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
- drops to **¥4,659/month** for 70+ head households [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)

**Demand outlook to 2050 (likely direction):**
- **Headwind:** advancing age reduces apparel purchasing frequency; also mobility constraints reduce shopping trips (unless e-commerce compensates).
- **Offsetting forces:** some seniors pursue “active ageing” lifestyles; cohort effects (today’s 50s/60s) may sustain higher fashion spend than older cohorts.
- **Net:** in many scenarios, **per-household real apparel spend declines**, even if niche “silver fashion” segments grow.

**Market-size implication:** apparel is the **smallest** of your four categories in elderly budgets; even modest declines in per-household spend can flatten or reduce the category despite more elderly households.

### 7.2 Food (at-home + out-of-home, and the rise of delivery / prepared meals)

**Current reality (FIES):**
- Food is the **largest** line item: **¥63,876/month** for 65+ head households [[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls)
- Eating out (meals outside home) is much lower for older households (e.g., 70+ head ¥5,631 vs overall ¥11,559 in 2023 table) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)

**Structural shift: from restaurant eating → “home-based convenience”**
A key measurable growth vector is **healthy meal delivery / care meals**:
- Japan’s “medical meal service and healthy meal delivery market” (hospital meals + care home meals + healthy meal delivery) was **¥2.4096T in FY2024**, and forecast to reach **¥2.5122T by FY2029** [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857).
- Within that market, hospital meals are expected to shrink, while care home meals and healthy meal delivery gain share [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857).
- The report highlights growth and competitive entry into **frozen meal delivery for the elderly**, reflecting both user convenience and provider efficiency [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857).

**Drivers to 2050:**
- More elderly **living alone** [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)[[37]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_PR.pdf) → higher demand for small-portion, nutritionally managed, easy-to-prepare food.
- More **85+** [[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx) → stronger demand for soft foods, therapeutic diets, assisted feeding contexts.
- Inflation risk: food CPI has been volatile and high recently (food CPI group index 125.8 in 2025 vs 2020=100) [[50]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/cpi/158c.html), which can shift consumption toward cheaper staples, private label, and frozen.

**Market-size implication:** Food remains the largest elderly-consumption category; within it, the most dynamic “incremental” opportunities are in:
- **healthy meal delivery**, **frozen prepared meals**, and
- **care-linked nutrition services** [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857).

### 7.3 Housing (rent/ownership costs, utilities, and repairs/renovation for ageing-in-place)

**Current reality (FIES):**
- “Housing” (narrow) for 65+ head households: **¥14,817/month** [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
- but note: older households have very high homeownership (around **90%** for older head ages in FIES tables) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls), which depresses measured “housing” outlays because rent is low and **imputed rent is excluded** from household consumption.
- Utilities are large and persistent: **¥20,534/month** for 65+ head households [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).

**Repairs & maintenance is notable in elderly budgets**
FIES shows elderly households spending meaningfully on **repairs & maintenance** (a proxy for renovation/maintenance needs):
- 65+ head households (2023): **¥10,143/month** on repairs & maintenance [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).
- This remains high even for 70+ [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).

**Drivers to 2050:**
- Elderly living alone rises sharply [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)[[37]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_PR.pdf) → more “safety retrofit” demand (handrails, anti-slip, step elimination), home monitoring, and small repairs (rather than large remodeling).
- Rising **85+** population [[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx) → higher probability of accessibility modifications or transitions into assisted living (not directly captured in “housing” if funded/recorded differently).
- Inflation & energy policy: utilities spending is sensitive to energy prices; CPI utilities components have been volatile (fuel/light/water group index 116.9 in 2025 vs 2020=100) [[50]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/cpi/158c.html).

**Market-size implication:** Measured “housing” outlays may not explode because ownership is high, but **(a)** utilities and **(b)** repairs/maintenance and “aging-in-place services” can be large and persistent markets.

### 7.4 Transportation (and communications as the connected companion category)

**Current reality (FIES):**
- 65+ head households: **¥27,041/month** for transportation & communication [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
  - transport-only: **¥18,527/month** (public + private) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)
- Transportation & communication declines sharply at 70+ compared to 60–69 [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls), consistent with reduced driving/travel.

**Drivers to 2050:**
- Ageing within elderly (more 75+/85+) [[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx) will likely reduce:
  - private vehicle use and “private transportation” spending,
  - long-distance discretionary travel (though some healthy seniors still travel).
- But there is offset potential:
  - substitution from private driving to **paid mobility services**, assisted transport, and delivery logistics embedded in retail and food.
- Communications are increasingly essential (smartphones, data plans, devices). In FIES, “communication” remains a meaningful monthly spend even for elderly [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).

**Market-size implication:** “Traditional transport spend” may face a **per-household decline**, but **mobility-as-a-service** (paid rides, assisted mobility) and **communication/digital connectivity** can partially offset.

## 8) Scenario market sizing (behavioral change + macro uncertainty)

Because 2020–2050 is long, it’s best to frame elderly market size as a **range** rather than a point estimate. Below is a pragmatic scenario structure consistent with what official sources imply about uncertainty in demographics and prices [[1]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/e/zenkoku_e2023/pp2023e_Summary.pdf)[[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf):

### 8.1 Scenario definitions (real per-household spend trajectory)

Starting from the 2023 FIES 65+ head basket [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)[[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls):

1) **Baseline (“mix ageing” headwind offsets cohort wealth”)**  
   - Real per-elderly-household spend is roughly **flat to slightly down** over time, because the share of 75+/85+ grows [[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx), and 70+ households spend less than 60–69 [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls), partially offset by cohort wealth and services substitution.
   - Practical modeling shorthand: **0% real growth** in the category totals, but **mix shifts** inside categories (more convenience food, more home services, fewer discretionary trips).

2) **Downside (“essentials crowd-out + older-elderly concentration”)**  
   - Higher inflation/energy/food costs [[50]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/cpi/158c.html) + budget constraints + higher care-related burdens reduce discretionary real spending, especially clothing and transport.
   - Modeling shorthand: **-0.5%/year real** per-household in discretionary categories; food/utility real spend stable.

3) **Upside (“healthy longevity + digital enablement + services innovation”)**  
   - Better health at older ages, expanded work participation, and innovation in senior services support stable consumption and shift spending into paid services.
   - Modeling shorthand: **+0.3%/year real** per-household in services-linked categories (food solutions, communications, home services), flat in clothing.

### 8.2 What that does to market size by 2050 (directionally)

Remember: the **number of 65+ head households rises ~15%** from 2020 to 2050 [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx). Therefore:

- If **real per-household spend is flat**, the market grows ~15% by volume (as shown in Section 5.3).
- If per-household real spend declines, totals can flatten.
- If per-household real spend increases modestly in service categories, total market can grow materially even with a flat household count after 2040.

Because your question is category-specific, the “most sensitive” categories are:

- **Clothing:** highest risk of long-run real decline as the elderly pool becomes older.
- **Transportation:** risk of decline in private transportation spend, but potential substitution into paid mobility assistance.
- **Food:** relatively resilient; composition shifts toward prepared/managed nutrition and delivery [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857).
- **Housing (repairs/utilities):** utilities and repairs/maintenance can remain resilient; aging-in-place creates steady demand [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).

## 9) Cross-checks, benchmarks, and key limitations (so you can use this responsibly)

### 9.1 Benchmark: senior consumption is already near half of household consumption in earlier estimates
A widely cited synthesis based on older survey/national accounts work estimated “senior consumption” (household head 60+) at **¥115T in 2014**, ~48% of personal consumption (excluding imputed rent) [[43]](https://www.nippon.com/en/in-depth/a04901/). While dated and based on a different definition and year, it supports the qualitative conclusion: **Japan is already a senior-led consumption society**, and this intensifies through 2050 given the projected age structure [[2]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-1.xlsx)[[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx)[[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).

### 9.2 Main methodological limitations of this market model
1) **Household-head proxy**: Not identical to “all spending by individuals aged 65+.”  
2) **Institutional consumption**: Spending for people in facilities may be recorded differently (public/private payers; household surveys may under-represent institutionalized elderly).  
3) **Imputed rent exclusion**: Housing consumption in national accounts includes imputed rent; FIES household “housing” does not fully capture the economic consumption value of owner-occupied housing. That’s why “housing” looks small vs food.  
4) **Category mapping**: “Transportation & communication” is a combined group in FIES major categories; I provided a “transport-only” view using public+private transportation sub-lines [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls).  
5) **Price vs volume**: Long-run nominal market sizes depend heavily on inflation assumptions; I therefore separated real vs nominal and anchored inflation scenarios to official assumption sets [[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf).

## 10) Actionable conclusions for an “elderly market” strategy (2020–2050)

1) **Japan will have ~39 million elderly (65+) through 2050**, but the internal shift is decisive:  
   **75+ rises to ~24.3m by 2050**, and **85+ stays near ~9.6m** (after peaking around 2040) [[2]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-1.xlsx)[[3]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-zenkoku/j/zenkoku2023/db_zenkoku2023/s_tables/1-9a.xlsx)[[6]](https://www.stat.go.jp/data/topics/pdf/topics146.pdf).  
   Designing for “65+” generically will miss the dominant growth in needs.

2) **The number of elderly-headed households rises to ~24.0m by 2050**, and **elderly living alone rises strongly** (elderly single-person households to ~10.84m) [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).  
   This structurally favors: small-pack goods, prepared meals, delivery, home safety services, and simplified mobility.

3) Using current spending patterns (FIES 2023) as a baseline and scaling by projected elderly households, the **annual “elderly household market” (65+ head households)** in real terms is on the order of:
   - **Food:** ~¥16.1T (2020) → ~¥18.4T (2050)
   - **Transport & communication:** ~¥6.8T → ~¥7.8T
   - **Utilities:** ~¥5.2T → ~¥5.9T
   - **Housing (narrow):** ~¥3.7T → ~¥4.3T
   - **Clothing:** ~¥1.3T → ~¥1.5T  
   (Real 2023-yen basis; household-head method) [[19]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s04.xls)[[21]](https://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/sousetai/2023n/zuhyou/s09.xls)[[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx).

4) **Food is the most robust category**, with the clearest evidence of growth in “delivery/managed nutrition” ecosystems: the **medical meal + healthy meal delivery** market is already ~**¥2.41T (FY2024)** and forecast ~**¥2.51T (FY2029)**, with notable momentum in **frozen meal delivery for the elderly** [[46]](https://www.yanoresearch.com/press/press.php/3857). This aligns strongly with the projected rise in elderly living alone [[36]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_gaiyo_hyox_20240412_DL.xlsx)[[37]](https://www.ipss.go.jp/pp-ajsetai/j/HPRJ2024/hprj2024_PR.pdf).

5) **Nominal 2050 market sizes** are highly inflation-dependent. Using official long-run inflation assumption sets, nominalization from “real” to 2050 yen can plausibly range from ~**+11%** (0.4% long-run inflation case) to **+73%** (2.0% case) vs 2023 yen levels [[52]](https://www.mhlw.go.jp/content/12506000/001242735.pdf). Your commercial plan should therefore treat “nominal market growth” carefully and focus on **real volume, mix, and margin structure**.
